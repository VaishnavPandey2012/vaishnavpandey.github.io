Biography

I grew up taking apart the family computer to see what was inside it, which turned into a decade-long habit of taking apart products instead. I studied mechanical engineering, which taught me almost nothing about software and everything about how systems fail — a lesson that has served me well in both disciplines.

My first real job was as employee #6 at a logistics startup, where I wrote code, sat in on sales calls, and drew wireframes on the same whiteboard in the same afternoon. That's still roughly how I like to work: close to the product, close to the customer, and close to the code.

Today I split my time between founding my own ventures and advising early-stage teams on product and engineering strategy. I care about software that respects the time of the person using it — fast, honest about what it can and can't do, and quietly well made.

How I like to work

I default to shipping a rough version and learning from real usage rather than perfecting a plan in a document. I write most of my own first prototypes, review every design before it ships, and I'm still happiest when I'm the one debugging production at 1am — it keeps me honest about what I ask engineering teams to own.

I'm drawn to teams that are small enough to move without a committee and serious enough to sweat the details other companies skip: onboarding copy, empty states, the fourth click in a flow nobody else tested.
